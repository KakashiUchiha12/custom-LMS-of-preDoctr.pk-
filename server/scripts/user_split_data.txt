PhotosynthesisThese questions focus on the synthesis of organic compounds using light energy, pigment structures, and the Calvin and C4 cycles.
56017 (Melvin Calvin): Carbon fixation. He mapped the path of carbon in photosynthesis (the Calvin Cycle). Shutterstock
56018 (Composition): Willstatter and Stahi. They were the pioneering chemists who determined the empirical formulas for chlorophyll.
56019 (Non-polar part): Phytol. This is a long hydrophobic hydrocarbon tail that anchors the chlorophyll molecule into the thylakoid membrane.
56020 (Porphyrin size): 15 Ångstrom square. This is the approximate size of the hydrophilic "head" of the chlorophyll molecule.
56021 (Activity): Sunlight. While specific wavelengths like red and blue are effective, full spectrum sunlight provides the maximum energy for all pigments.
56022 (Intensity): Increase till feedback inhibition. Rate rises with light until the plant's metabolic enzymes are saturated or limited by CO2.
56023 (Available light): 50%. Roughly half of total solar radiation is Photosynthetically Active Radiation (PAR) that reaches the Earth's surface.
56024 (Energy): 90; 10. Blue light has shorter wavelengths and higher energy (kcal) per photon compared to red light.
56025 (Converter): Sugar cane. It is a C4 plant with a highly efficient mechanism for capturing CO2 and converting it into biomass.
56026 (Cyclic): Photosystem I. This process involves only PS I to produce ATP without generating NADPH or O2. Shutterstock Explore 
56027 (CO2 consumption): 1/4 of the CO2 of air. This refers to the efficiency and concentration gradients land plants maintain for hexose synthesis.
56028 (ATP Requirement): 18 for C3; 30 for C4. C4 plants require extra ATP to pump CO2 into bundle sheath cells to avoid photorespiration.
56029 (O2/CO2 Ratio): 1. According to the balanced equation of photosynthesis, one molecule of O2 is released for every molecule of CO2 fixed.
56030 (Warburg’s effect): C3 plants. High oxygen levels inhibit photosynthesis in C3 plants because Rubisco starts fixing O2 instead of CO2.
56031 (C4 Plant): Maize. Corn (Maize) utilizes the C4 pathway to stay productive in hot, dry conditions.
56032 (Water plant): O2 bubbles. Submerged plants like Hydrilla release visible oxygen bubbles in water, making the rate easy to measure.
56033 (Formation): Fe²⁺ and Mg²⁺. Magnesium is the central atom of the chlorophyll ring, while Iron is a vital catalyst for its synthesis.
56034 (Translocation): Sucrose. Sugars are moved through the phloem as sucrose because it is chemically stable and highly soluble.
56035 (Q10): Temperature coefficient. It represents the factor by which the rate of a reaction increases with a 10°C rise in temperature.
56036 (Photorespiration): High O2 and low CO2. This environment forces Rubisco to act as an oxygenase, wasting energy.
56037 (Quinones): Mobile electron carriers. Small molecules like Plastoquinone move electrons between protein complexes in the thylakoid membrane.
56038 (PGA derivation): RuBP + CO2. The 3-carbon PGA is formed when the 5-carbon RuBP combines with 1-carbon CO2 and splits.
56039 (3 CO2 Fixation): 9 ATP and 6 NADPH2. For every CO2 fixed, 3 ATP and 2 NADPH are consumed in the Calvin Cycle.
56040 (Stroma enzyme): RuBP carboxylase (Rubisco). This is the most abundant protein on Earth, found in the chloroplast stroma.
56041 (Thylakoids only): CO2 assimilating enzymes absent. While thylakoids do the light reactions, the enzymes for sugar synthesis (dark reactions) are in the stroma.
56042 (Solar converter): Sugarcane. It has one of the highest known rates of solar energy conversion into chemical energy.
56043 (Requirement): Quantum requirement. The specific number of photons (usually 8-10) needed to evolve one molecule of oxygen.
56044 (Calvin Cycle): Reductive carboxylation. CO2 is fixed (carboxylation) and then reduced using electrons from NADPH.
56045 (Hill reaction): None. The Hill reaction specifically demonstrates that O2 can be released from water in the absence of CO2 fixation.
56046 (Max Photosynthesis): Red. Chlorophyll absorbs red light very efficiently for the photosynthetic process.
56047 (Kranz anatomy): Bundle sheath agranal. In C4 plants, mesophyll cells have grana for light reactions; bundle sheath cells lack grana to focus on the Calvin Cycle.
56048 (Limiting factors): CO2, light, temperature. The rate of photosynthesis is governed by whichever of these factors is at its minimum.
56049/56056 (Formula): C55H72O5N4Mg. This is the specific molecular formula for Chlorophyll a.
56050 (Absent in): Bacteria. Photosynthetic bacteria use bacteriochlorophyll rather than the Chlorophyll a found in plants and algae.
56051 (Start point): Phosphoglycolate. When Rubisco fixes oxygen, the first product is a 2-carbon molecule called phosphoglycolate.
56052 (C4 Error): Characteristic of C4. C4 plants have evolved to almost entirely eliminate photorespiration, unlike C3 plants.
56053 (Enzyme): PEP carboxylase. This enzyme has a much higher affinity for CO2 than Rubisco and is the first fixer in sugarcane.
56054 (Plants in rooms): CO2 release. At night, photosynthesis stops, but respiration continues, meaning plants release CO2 and consume O2.
56055 (Scientist): Blackman. He proposed that when a process depends on multiple factors, the rate is limited by the slowest factor.
56057 (Electrons): 24. Splitting water to produce 6 O2 molecules and 1 glucose requires the transfer of 24 electrons.
56059 (Decarboxylation): Mesophyll cells. In C4 plants, C4 acids are made in mesophyll cells and then moved to bundle sheath cells for decarboxylation.
56060 (Gas exchange): Release O2, absorb CO2. This is the fundamental gas exchange of the photosynthetic process.
56061 (85% activity): Phytoplanktons. The vast majority of the Earth's oxygen production and carbon fixation occurs in the oceans.
RespirationThese questions focus on the breakdown of glucose, energy release (ATP), and metabolic pathways like Glycolysis and the Krebs Cycle.
56058 (Compensation point): Rate of photo = rate of resp. At this specific light intensity, the CO2 absorbed equals the CO2 released.
56062 (Process): Catabolic. Respiration breaks down complex molecules into simpler ones to release energy.
56063 (Metabolism): Anabolic and Catabolic. Metabolism is the sum of all building-up (anabolic) and breaking-down (catabolic) reactions.
56064 (Energy source): Respiration. This is the cellular process that converts biochemical energy from nutrients into ATP.
56065 (Reaction type): Exergonic. Energy is released during the breakdown of glucose, which is then captured in ATP bonds.
56066 (Food state): Oxidized. During respiration, glucose loses electrons (and hydrogens) to become CO2.
56067 (Main substrate): Glucose. It is the primary fuel that cells prefer to oxidize for immediate energy.
56068 (ATP formation): Water. The condensation reaction between ADP and Pi releases a water molecule (dehydration synthesis).
56069 (Occurrence): All of these. Every living cell, whether prokaryotic or eukaryotic, must respire to survive.
56070 (Complete oxidation): Aerobic. Only in the presence of oxygen is glucose fully broken down into CO2 and H2O.
56071 (Energy yield): Aerobic. Aerobic respiration produces much more ATP (approx. 36-38) compared to anaerobic (2 ATP).
56072 (Fermentation): Absence of oxygen. This is an anaerobic pathway used to regenerate NAD+ so glycolysis can continue.
56073 (First stage): Glycolysis. This is the universal first step of glucose breakdown, occurring in all types of respiration. gstraub
56074 (Conversion): Glycolysis. One glucose molecule is split into two molecules of pyruvic acid.
56075 (Pyruvic count): 2. The 6-carbon glucose is halved into two 3-carbon pyruvate molecules.
56076 (Carbon count): 3. Pyruvic acid ($C_3H_4O_3$) is a three-carbon organic acid.
56077 (Location): Cytoplasm. Glycolysis does not require organelles and happens in the fluid part of the cell.
56078 (Oxygen used): 0. Glycolysis is an anaerobic process; oxygen is only needed later in the mitochondria.
56079 (CO2 produced): 0. CO2 is only released during the Link Reaction and the Krebs Cycle, not during glycolysis.
56080 (Final acceptor): Oxygen. In the electron transport chain, oxygen picks up protons and electrons to form water.
56081 (Not in anaerobic): Acetyl CoA. Pyruvate only converts to Acetyl CoA if it is entering the mitochondria for aerobic respiration.
56082 (Product): ATP. Adenosine Diphosphate plus a phosphate group yields Adenosine Triphosphate.
56083 (DHAP): 3 C compound. This is one of the two 3-carbon sugars formed when Fructose-1,6-bisphosphate is split.
56084 (ATP consumed): 2. Two ATP molecules are "invested" at the start of glycolysis to activate the glucose molecule.
56085 (NADH to NAD): Oxidation. The loss of hydrogen/electrons from NADH to become NAD+ is an oxidation reaction.
